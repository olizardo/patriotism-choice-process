# Introduction

Which groups are more likely to experience increased feelings of
national pride after the experience of a threat to nation? In this
paper, I use survey data on a representative sample of Americans
collected before and after the events of 9/11 to shed light on this
question. This "natural experiment" allows me adjudicate between two
competing hypotheses regarding the socio-demographic distribution of
feeling of national pride after the experience of a threat to the
nation.

Theories in the tradition of the "authoritarian personality" conceive of
national pride as reflecting a form "irrational conservatism." These
theories predict that members of low status groups will be much more
likely to experience renewed feelings of national pride after the
experience of a threat to the nation. From this perspective, a threat to
the nation is seen as endangering the "ontological security" (Giddens
1984) of persons whose lives are characterized by routine and alienating
work and who are disengaged from established institutions.

Theories of attachment to collectivities as a nested group on the other
hand (Lawler 1992; Mueller and Lawler 1999), point to a different
dynamic, and lead to a clearly divergent empirical implication.
According to the choice-process approach, individuals are more likely to
experience affective attachment to a larger group within which other
groups that they belong to is nested (such as the nation), only to the
extent that they are able to *in fact* experience freedom and autonomy
in their everyday dealings, and to the extent that this autonomy and
freedom is *attributed* to membership both in the more proximate groups
that they are part of, and to the larger unit within which these groups
are nested (Lawler 1992). Given the fact that high status individuals
are more likely to be in structural positions that in fact afford
autonomy and self-discretion (Kohn and Schooler 1969, 1978) and given
that they are more likely to in fact belong to "nested-subgroups", such
as voluntary associations (Bell and Force 1956; Freeman, Novak, and
Reeder 1957: 528; Janoski and Wilson 1996; Knoke 1986: 4; McPherson
1981: 718; Scott 1957; Tomeh 1973), within which feelings of attachment
to super-ordinate collectivities can be fostered and made salient, the
choice-process theory predicts that it will be high-status individuals,
who will be more likely to experience renewed feelings of national pride
following a threat to the nation.

The results are consistent with the implications of the choice-process
approach. After 9/11 we can see an overall increase in feelings of pride
directed toward the United States as a national collectivity.
Furthermore, it is more educated individuals and those employed in high
status occupations who are more likely to have experienced increased
feelings of national pride. The effect of education and occupational
status furthermore is largely mediated by the number of voluntary
association memberships, a result that is concordant with the proposed
choice-process mechanism.

# The origins of feelings of pride and national attachment: Two approaches

## Feelings of national pride as an affective "consolation prize"

Standard approaches in social psychology geared to the explanation of
individual differences in affective attachment to nations as
collectivities---especially those spikes in expressions of national
pride that follow such traumatic events as the events of 9/11---usually
point factors associated with the (unconscious or consciously
recognized) emotional needs that such feelings of national pride provide
for the person. Thus, Adorno, Frnkel-Brunswik, Levinson, and Sanford
(1950) in their classic study of the "authoritarian personality" noted
how persons who scored high on their "F-scale" were also prone to
express "irrational" feelings of pride and "blind" attachment for the
nation conceived as a solidary collectivity---and possibly, they
surmised, be attracted to authoritarian demagogues expressing similar
feelings. Most importantly for the purposes of this study, Adorno et al
(1950) found that among those who they classified as "authoritarian",
feelings of pride and attachment to the nation *increased* whenever with
the presence of a real or perceived threat to the integrity of the
nation.

Subsequent research in political psychology has confirmed this linkage
between increases feelings of patriotism or national attachment and the
perceived presence of a threat (references; Perrin 2005: 168). In a
recent study of the same effect following the 9/11 attacks, Lavine,
Lodge, and Freitas (2005) find that...

-   Social Identity Theory and Self-Categorization Theory can be
    considered "need" based theories

    -   Self-enhancement motive leads individuals to become affectively
        attached to prestigious national collectivities

    -   Self-identity and self-categorization is attained by defining
        the ingroup in relation to the outgroup

    -   Affective attachment to the nation usually accompanied by
        feelings of *superiority* and *dominance* in relation to other
        national groups

National pride is usually correlated with manifestations of "blind
patriotism" (Schatz, Staub and Lavine 1999)

"Patriotic" respondents much more likely (Sullivan et al 1992):

to support militarism

to reject national criticism

to show uncritical support for government policies

to be ideologically conservative

What is the main empirical implication of conceiving of feelings of
national pride in the manner outlined above? If feelings of national
pride are an expression of either "authoritarianism" or "irrational
conservatism" then we should find it *overrepresented* among those
groups marked by the socio-demographic characteristics typically
associated with these set of cognitive and affective orientations. The
bulk of the research shows that this refers to low status, low education
groups, typically alienated from mainstream institutions and typically
disempowered in their everyday intercourse, especially at the workplace
(Collins 1986; other references). This implies that:

Hypothesis 1: *After 9/11, feelings of national pride in the United
States increase at a faster rate among low education, low occupational
status groups.*

## Feelings of national pride as an affective "nested attachment"

"The *responsibility principle...*stipulates that those subgroups or
groups perceived to be the most empowering enjoy the strongest affective
attachments" (Lawler 1992: 333)*.*

"...if citizens view the state as the primary source of empowerment in
the form of new social, economic and political options and rights, then
states become more important sources of positive emotion and are subject
to stronger affective attachments" (Lawler 1992: 333)*.*

"Based on the *proximal rule*, actors develop stronger affective ties to
subgroups within a social system rather than to the social
system...Larger collectivities that foster choice and freedom among
members still enhance positive actor-to-collectivity affective bonds,
but less so than those to the nested subgroups" (Lawler 1992: 334)*.*

"A *distal rule* simply inverts the proximal rule: The more distant,
remote and all-encompassing collectivity is the primary source of
individual empowerment and constraint. Choice processes that generate
positive emotion will strengthen emotional ties to the larger
collectivity more than to the smaller subgroups nested within it...A
distal rule is especially likely when the larger group closely monitors
or regulates subgroups...when collective rituals and symbols are
frequent enough to make the larger collectivity highly salient...and
when social transformations create a larger social units from smaller
ones...Use of the proximal rule for positive emotion and the distal rule
for negative emotions is contingent on whether the subgroup or the
larger group is more salient to actors..." (Lawler 1992: 334)*.*

We use a modified version of Lawler's (1992) choice-process theory. Our
main modification of the theory consists of relaxing the assumption that
negative emotions invariably result in weakened attachments to the
superordinate group. Instead we suggest that everyday negative emotions
may lead to a disattachment from proximal subgroups and an increase
attachment to superordinate groups, such as the "imagined community"
(Anderson 1991) of the nation-state.

Persons located in less advantageous position in the social structure
should be less likely to be attached to the groups that they are more
likely to be a part of, since these groups---i.e. highly constraining
lower service occupations---are unlikely to provide them with autonomy
and freedom to determine their courses of action. Thus, *we should
expect that attachment to large scale imagined communities that promise
empowerment and choice should be largest among the most materially and
culturally dispossessed groups in society.* Conversely, it should be
weakest among high status members, *in particular members who are
members of alternative groups that reaffirm the collective sources of
their status group membership such as professional associations*.
