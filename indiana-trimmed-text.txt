Where does national pride come from?  The needs based model
Social Identity Theory and Self-Categorization Theory can be considered “need” based theories
Self-enhancement motive leads individuals to become affectively attached to prestigious national collectivities
Self-identity and self-categorization is attained by defining the 
ingroup
 in relation to the 
outgroup
Affective attachment to the nation usually accompanied by feelings of 
superiority
 and 
dominance
 in relation to other national groups
Lawler’s choice-process theory	
National groups will be the object of strong positive affective attachments to the extent that they are 
perceived as responsible 
for giving the person
A sense of autonomy and freedom
A sense of control over their environment (self-efficacy)
Discussion and Conclusion
From the choice-process perspective, after 9/11 lower status individuals less likely to increase their levels of national pride than high status individuals due to the fact that
They are less likely to experience autonomy in their everyday affairs, and thus less likely to be part of the affective choice process
Low status individuals less likely to be embedded in nested groups (low “social capital”)
Where does national pride come from?  The needs based model
Individuals become attached to national groups in the very same way that they become attached to any other group
Attachment to national groups is likely if the group is perceived as meeting emotional needs
Most important among these needs are those related to self-esteem, prestige and a sense of efficacy and belonging
Where does national pride come from?  Psychological social psychology
In the following national pride will be considered an example of 
affective attachment 
to a collectivity
Two major set of perspectives in psychology
The “psychological needs” view
Affective attachment to groups rooted in the ability of those groups to satisfy individual needs for self-esteem, identity, prestige, etc.
The “irrational” conservatism view
Affective attachment to the nation as unquestioning submission and loyalty

Discussion and Conclusion
The choice-process perspective predicts that
Efficacious and autonomous action in socially structured contexts increases affective attachment to the national group under a new attribution regime (post 9/11)
Post 9/11, it was precisely those individuals more likely to experience autonomy in their day to day behavior that increased their attachment to the national collectivity
Embeddedness in nested groups accounts for the association between markers of status and autonomy (i.e. education) and higher degrees of affective attachment to the national group after 9/11

Discussion and Conclusion
After 9/11 U.S. appears to have switched from a “local” to a “national” affective attachment rule
Psychological perspectives on nationalism don’t help us in predicting 
which
 individuals were more likely to experience national pride in the most recent period
Needs-based approaches predict that those with the most dire needs for belongingness, self-esteem and prestige (low status groups) should increase their attachment
Irrational conservatism approaches predict that those with high levels of nationalism should have increased their attachment
Neither is the case for the rise in feelings of national pride in post 9/11 U.S.
